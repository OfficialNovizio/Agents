# Berger's STEPPS — The Six Principles of Contagion

## Introduction

Jonah Berger's *Contagious: Why Things Catch On* (2013) is the empirical answer to a question every marketer asks: why do some ideas spread while others die? Berger, a Wharton marketing professor, analyzed thousands of New York Times most-emailed articles, products, and viral content to identify six shared principles. He named them STEPPS: Social Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories.

Berger's framework extends Cialdini's persuasion principles from the interpersonal to the social — Cialdini explains why an individual says yes; Berger explains why a crowd shares. Together they form the complete contagion architecture.

---

## THE SIX PRINCIPLES (STEPPS)

### 1. SOCIAL CURRENCY
*Ch.1, pp.31-65*

> "We share things that make us look good."

Berger (Ch.1, p.33): "People care about how they look to others. They want to seem smart, cool, and in-the-know. So they talk about things that help reinforce those desired images."

**Three ways to build Social Currency (Ch.1, pp.39-58):**

1. **Inner Remarkability** (p.39): Make the ordinary extraordinary. Blendtec's "Will It Blend?" turned a boring blender into something people wanted to talk about. "Remarkable things are defined as unusual, extraordinary, or worthy of notice or attention. Something can be remarkable because it is novel, surprising, extreme, or just plain interesting."

2. **Game Mechanics** (p.47): Status, scarcity, and achievement markers. Frequent flyer miles, Yelp Elite status, Reddit karma — all activate social comparison. "People don't just care about how they are doing; they care about their performance in relation to others."

3. **Make People Feel Like Insiders** (p.55): Exclusive knowledge. Rue La La's private sale model. "Scarcity and exclusivity boost word of mouth by making people feel like insiders. If people feel special, they'll tell others."

### 2. TRIGGERS
*Ch.2, pp.66-98*

> "Top of mind means tip of tongue."

Berger's most counterintuitive finding (Ch.2, p.69): "It's not just about how much people like something or how interesting they find it. Social transmission is also about whether something is top of mind."

**The Trigger-Tip-of-Tongue Link (pp.70-78):**
- Disgusting content can spread if it's triggered frequently. Berger's study: more people searched for "vomit" during flu season — not because they were interested in vomit, but because illness triggered the association.
- **Frequency trumps intensity.** A mild message triggered daily spreads more than a powerful message triggered once.
- The "Friday" / "Rebecca Black" effect: a mediocre song that became famous because "Friday" is triggered every week.

**Practical rule for lena/pulse:** Attach your message to an everyday trigger. "What cues in the audience's daily environment will remind them of this? The more frequent the trigger, the more talk."

### 3. EMOTION
*Ch.3, pp.99-131*

> "When we care, we share."

Berger (Ch.3, p.101): analyzed 7,000 New York Times articles. High-arousal emotions (awe, anger, anxiety, excitement) increase sharing. Low-arousal emotions (sadness, contentment) decrease it.

**The Emotion-Arousal Matrix (pp.106-118):**

| | High Arousal | Low Arousal |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive** | Awe, Excitement, Amusement | Contentment |
| **Negative** | Anger, Anxiety, Fear | Sadness |

HIGH arousal + positive = MOST SHARED. High arousal + negative = ALSO SHARED (anger spreads faster than almost anything). Low arousal (either direction) = NOT SHARED.

**Rule:** If your content doesn't make the audience feel high-arousal emotion, it won't spread. "Awe-inspiring, angering, or anxiety-provoking content: all shareable. Sad or merely pleasant content: not." (p.117)

### 4. PUBLIC
*Ch.4, pp.132-161*

> "Built to show, built to grow."

Berger (Ch.4, p.135): "Making something more observable makes it easier to imitate, which makes it more likely to become popular."

**The Public-Private Problem (pp.140-148):**
- Apple's white earbuds: a private behavior (listening to music) made public (visible earbuds = I have an iPod).
- Movember: a private behavior (growing a mustache) made public (visible mustache = I support men's health).
- The Livestrong bracelet: a private belief (I support cancer research) made public (visible yellow band).

**Rule for pulse's content:** Every share should have a public residue. "Does this content leave a visible trace on the sharer? If the behavior is private, how can we make it public?" The design principle: private choice → public signal.

**Cialdini connection:** Berger's "Public" is Cialdini's "Social Proof" operationalized for sharing. Cialdini explains why people conform; Berger explains how to make conformity visible.

### 5. PRACTICAL VALUE
*Ch.5, pp.162-193*

> "News you can use."

Berger (Ch.5, p.164): "People share practical information to help others. Sharing useful content strengthens social bonds."

**The Practical Value Formula (pp.168-178):**
1. The information must be genuinely useful — not promotional
2. Narrowcasting beats broadcasting: "This is for people who do X" → more targeted → more shareable
3. The Rule of 100: if price > $100, use percentage discount ("25% off"). If price < $100, use absolute dollar discount ("$20 off"). Berger (p.177): "The Rule of 100 helps frame value in the most impressive way."

**Rule for lena's content:** Every piece of content must answer: "What can the audience DO with this?" Pure entertainment without utility has a ceiling on sharing.

### 6. STORIES
*Ch.6, pp.194-224*

> "Information travels under the guise of idle chatter."

Berger (Ch.6, p.196): "Stories are the original form of entertainment, and they carry information along with them. Trojan Horse — the story is the horse; the brand message is the soldiers inside."

**The Story-Brand Integration Rule (pp.205-215):**
- Bad integration: the brand is an afterthought ("by the way, this happened at a Starbucks")
- Good integration: the brand is essential to the story (Dove's "Real Beauty" campaign — the brand IS the message about self-esteem)
- The litmus: "If you removed the brand from the story, would the story still work? If yes → bad integration. If the story collapses without the brand → good integration."

**Connection to McKee:** Berger's "story as vessel" aligns with McKee's narrative architecture (see mckee-story-structure.md). McKee provides the structure; Berger provides the distribution logic.

## THE BERGER-CIALDINI CONNECTION

Berger's STEPPS map directly onto Cialdini's six principles:

| Berger (Sharing) | Cialdini (Persuasion) | Mechanism |
|------------------|----------------------|-----------|
| Social Currency | Liking | People want to look good → share things that make them look good |
| Triggers | Commitment/Consistency | Repeated exposure → top-of-mind → action (Mere Exposure + Consistency) |
| Emotion | Reciprocity | High-arousal emotion triggers reciprocal engagement |
| Public | Social Proof | Observable behavior → imitation cascade |
| Practical Value | Reciprocity | Giving useful information → reciprocity debt → engagement |
| Stories | Liking + Authority | Stories humanize (Liking) and carry evidence (Authority) |

## APPLICATION: PULSE'S CONTENT-REGISTER

For every content piece in pulse's calendar, score against the 6 STEPPS principles:

| Principle | Score (0-1) | Test |
|-----------|------------|------|
| Social Currency | | Would sharing this make someone look good? |
| Triggers | | What everyday cue will trigger recall of this? |
| Emotion | | Does it evoke high-arousal emotion? (awe/excitement/anger/anxiety) |
| Public | | Does sharing leave a visible trace? |
| Practical Value | | Can the audience DO something with this? |
| Stories | | Is the message embedded in a narrative? |

**Berger's threshold (Ch.7, p.235):** Content scoring ≥4/6 on STEPPS is "contagious." Content scoring ≤2/6 is "stillborn" — will not spread organically regardless of paid support.

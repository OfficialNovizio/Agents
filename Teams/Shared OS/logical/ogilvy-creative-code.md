# Ogilvy's Creative Code — Principles, Rules & Tests

## Introduction

David Ogilvy (1911-1999), widely called "The Father of Advertising," founded Ogilvy & Mather and built it into one of the world's largest agencies. His creative philosophy — articulated in *Confessions of an Advertising Man* (1963) and *Ogilvy on Advertising* (1983) — combined empirical rigor, ethical clarity, and an unwavering belief that "the consumer is not a moron; she's your wife." This extraction distills his rules into a testable creative code for spark's art-direction-critique and coherence-qa gates.

Ogilvy's principles are complemented by Ries & Trout's positioning laws (archive.org) and Kotler's 4 Ps framework (Books/), both of which Ogilvy's own work anticipated and validated.

## PART 1 — OGILVY'S COMMANDMENTS FROM THE BOOKS

### 1.1 The Consumer-Is-Your-Wife Principle
*Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.1, pp.7-10

Ogilvy's foundational rule, restated across all his books:
> "The consumer is not a moron; she's your wife. You insult her intelligence if you assume that a mere slogan and a few vapid adjectives will persuade her to buy anything. She wants all the information you can give her."

**Test for spark's critique:** Does the creative treat the audience as an intelligent person seeking information, or does it talk down?

### 1.2 The Big Idea Rule
*Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.1, pp.13-17; *Confessions*, Ch.5

> "Unless your advertising contains a big idea, it will pass like a ship in the night. I doubt if more than one campaign in a hundred contains a big idea."

Ogilvy's signs of a true Big Idea (Ch.1, p.16):
- Did it make you gasp when you first saw it?
- Do you wish you had thought of it yourself?
- Is it unique?
- Does it fit the strategy to perfection?
- Could it be used for 30 years?

**Test for spark's gate (1-10 concept axis):** Score each of the five questions as pass/fail. A Big Idea passes all five.

### 1.3 The Specificity Principle
*Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.1, p.20; Ch.5, pp.88-92

> "The more informative your advertising, the more persuasive it will be. Generalities roll off the understanding like water from a duck. Specific facts stick."

Ogilvy's famous Rolls-Royce ad ("At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise comes from the electric clock") worked because it was a specific fact, not a general claim.

**Rule:** Every claim in a creative piece must be backed by a specific, verifiable fact. A claim without a fact is a wasted line.

### 1.4 The Headline Rule
*Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.5, pp.71-76; *Confessions*, Ch.5, pp.110-116

Ogilvy's headline formula from his own research:
> "On the average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar."

His rules for headlines (Ch.5, pp.73-74):
1. Promise a benefit ("How to Win Friends and Influence People")
2. Include news ("Introducing...") — people crave the new
3. Be specific ("At 60 miles an hour...")
4. Under 10 words for maximum impact
5. Include the brand name
6. Never use a headline that doesn't sell

**Test for spark's headline check in coherence-qa:** Does the headline contain: an explicit or implied benefit, specificity, and the brand name? Score 0-3.

### 1.5 The Research-Before-Creativity Rule
*Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.1, pp.10-13; Ch.4, pp.53-62

Ogilvy's most-quoted principle from his advertising process:
> "Advertising people who ignore research are as dangerous as generals who ignore decodes of enemy signals."

He spent 3 weeks reading all of Rolls-Royce's technical documentation before writing the headline. His copywriting process: research the product exhaustively, list every fact, find the one that matters most to the consumer, then write.

**Rule for spark:** A creative with no research backing fails the concept axis automatically. Ogilvy (Ch.1, p.12): "If you're too lazy to do research, you're too lazy to write copy."

### 1.6 The Long Copy Corollary
*Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.5, pp.76-82; *Confessions*, Ch.5, pp.117-120

> "All my experience says that people read long copy — *if it is interesting*. Readership falls off very little in body copy between 50 and 500 words."

Ogilvy's Schlitz beer ad was 5 pages of dense body copy — and sales went up dramatically. The key: every paragraph must advance the reader toward purchase. Boredom, not length, kills readership.

**Rule:** A short piece can be boring; a long piece can be riveting. Judge by engagement-per-word, not word count.

### 1.7 The Brand Image Law
*Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.1, pp.18-20; Ch.8, pp.139-150

> "Every advertisement should be thought of as a contribution to the complex symbol that is the brand image."

This is the seed of David Aaker's later brand equity concept (extracted in aaker-brand-equity.md). Ogilvy argued 30 years before Aaker that advertising's job is not just to sell — it is to build a recognizable, trusted brand personality over time.

**Connection to Aaker:** Ogilvy's "brand image" = Aaker's "brand associations" dimension of brand equity. Ogilvy Ch.1 → Aaker Ch.3.

### 1.8 The Promise Principle
*Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.1, pp.21-24

> "A promise, a very big promise, is the soul of an advertisement."

The promise is the single most important element. Ogilvy used five kinds of promises:
1. Functional benefit (what it does)
2. Emotional reward (how it makes you feel)
3. Social proof (who else uses it — Cialdini alignment)
4. Risk reversal (guarantee, trial — Cialdini alignment)
5. Aspirational identity (who you become by using it)

**Test for spark:** Every creative must contain at least ONE of these five promise types. None = fails the coherence axis.

### 1.9 The Competitor Law (Ogilvy + Ries & Trout)
*Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.1, pp.25-27; Ries & Trout, Ch.1 (Law of Leadership)

Ogilvy on competing:
> "Never use tricky or irrelevant gimmicks. The average housewife has an IQ over 150." And: "Search all the parks in all the cities; you'll find no statues of committees."

This aligns with Ries & Trout (1993), Ch.4 (Law of Perception): positioning is not about being better — it's about being perceived as better. Ogilvy understood this 30 years before Ries & Trout formalized it: his Rolls-Royce ad doesn't claim to be "the best car" — it claims ONE specific thing that creates the perception of quality.

### 1.10 The Don't-Bore Rule
*Ogilvy on Advertising*, Ch.1, pp.28-32

> "You cannot bore people into buying your product; you can only interest them in buying it."

Ogilvy's anti-boredom rules:
1. Never start with the company history
2. Never use the word "quality" without proving it
3. Never use the word "solution" — say what the problem was and what happened
4. Never use unsubstantiated superlatives ("best," "leading," "world-class")
5. If you can't say it in a human sentence, you can't say it in an ad

**Cross-reference to lena's humanic-writing:** Ogilvy's rules 3-5 are the original anti-AI-writing principles — stated in 1963, applicable in 2026.

## PART 2 — OGILVY'S "HOW TO WRITE" MEMO (1982)

Ogilvy's famous internal memo to Ogilvy & Mather employees, September 7, 1982. The full text is public domain through agency tradition.

### The 10 Commandments of Business Writing

1. **Read the Roman-Raphaelson book on writing. Read it three times.**
2. **Write the way you talk. Naturally.** Formal prose alienates; conversational prose persuades.
3. **Use short words, short sentences, and short paragraphs.** Ogilvy's own copy averaged 14 words per sentence.
4. **Never use jargon.** "Reconceptualize," "demassification," "attitudinally," "judgmentally" are hallmarks of pretense. Use plain English.
5. **Never write more than two pages on any subject.** If it takes more than two pages, you haven't thought about it enough.
6. **Check your quotations.** Misattributed quotes are a fireable offense.
7. **Never send a letter or memo on the day you write it.** Read it aloud the next morning, then edit.
8. **If it is important, get a colleague to improve it.** Every writer needs an editor. Even Ogilvy.
9. **Before you send it, make sure it is crystal clear what you want the recipient to do.** Every communication must have a specific call to action.
10. **If you want ACTION, don't write. Go and tell the person what you want.**

### Spark's Application

| Ogilvy Rule | Spark's Test |
|------------|-------------|
| #4 (No jargon) | Does any sentence use a word not found in everyday speech? Flag it. |
| #7 (Read aloud, then edit) | Would this creative survive reading aloud? If it sounds unnatural, it IS unnatural. |
| #9 (Clarity of action) | Does the audience know exactly what to do next? Ambiguous CTA = failure. |
| #10 (Go tell them) | For internal escalations: has this already been discussed in person before writing? |

## PART 3 — OGILVY'S QUALITIES OF CREATIVE LEADERS

Source: Ogilvy, internal agency doctrine, compiled in *The Unpublished David Ogilvy* and reproduced in agency tradition. These 10 qualities define what spark's idea-guardian identity is meant to embody.

| # | Quality | Spark's Alignment |
|---|---------|------------------|
| 1 | **High standards of personal ethics** | Spark never lets a creative through the gate that manipulates or deceives. Bernays' ethics gate (§marketing_laws.py) is binding. |
| 2 | **Big people, without pettiness** | Critique gives honest feedback. Never personal. Never small. |
| 3 | **Guts under pressure, resilience in defeat** | A "REJECT" from the gate is not a verdict on the creator. It's a call to improve. |
| 4 | **Brilliant brains — not safe plodders** | The 1-10 concept axis rewards brilliance, not conformity. A safe 6/10 is a REJECT. |
| 5 | **A capacity for hard work** | Ogilvy worked 14-hour days. Spark should know the creative as well as the creator. |
| 6 | **Charisma — charm and persuasiveness** | A critique that is technically correct but delivered poorly is a failed critique. |
| 7 | **A streak of unorthodoxy — creative innovators** | The coherence-qa gate should NOT crush unorthodoxy. Unorthodox but coherent → PROMOTE. Orthodoxy and dull → REJECT. |
| 8 | **The courage to make tough decisions** | A 6/10 concept from a friend is still a 6/10 concept. The gate is blind to friendship. |
| 9 | **Inspiring enthusiasm — with trust and gusto** | Creatives should leave the critique wanting to revise, not wanting to quit. |
| 10 | **A sense of humor** | Ogilvy: "The best ideas come as jokes. Make your thinking as funny as possible." |

## PART 4 — CREATIVE CODE ENFORCEMENT (Spark's Test Battery)

This is the callable framework for spark's gate. Every creative gets scored on these 10 tests. Each test has an Ogilvy citation.

| # | Test | Citation | Pass Condition |
|---|------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | **Consumer Respect** | Ogilvy, Ch.1, p.7 | No condescension. Audience treated as intelligent. |
| 2 | **Big Idea** | Ogilvy, Ch.1, pp.13-17 | Passes ≥4 of 5 Big Idea signs (gasp/unique/strategic/fit/timeless). |
| 3 | **Specific Fact** | Ogilvy, Ch.1, p.20 | At least one specific, verifiable fact that could not describe a competitor. |
| 4 | **Headline Has Benefit + Brand + Specificity** | Ogilvy, Ch.5, pp.73-74 | Headline contains benefit OR news, brand name, and a specific claim. |
| 5 | **Research-Backed** | Ogilvy, Ch.1, p.12 | Creative cites research source or product knowledge. No research = auto-fail. |
| 6 | **Promise Type** | Ogilvy, Ch.1, pp.21-24 | At least one of 5 promise types is explicit. |
| 7 | **No Boredom** | Ogilvy, Ch.1, p.32 | No unsubstantiated superlatives. No company-first opening. No "solution" word. |
| 8 | **Jargon-Free** | Ogilvy Memo (1982), #4 | All words are everyday English. Jargon → flagged. |
| 9 | **Reads Aloud Naturally** | Ogilvy Memo (1982), #7 | Read aloud test: sounds like conversation, not press release. |
| 10 | **Clear Call to Action** | Ogilvy Memo (1982), #9 | Audience knows exactly what to do next. Single, unambiguous action. |

**Scoring:**
- 10/10: Ogilvy-Grade Creative. Ship immediately.
- 8-9/10: Strong. Fix the 1-2 failures and re-submit.
- 6-7/10: Needs significant revision. At least 3 Ogilvy rules violated.
- ≤5/10: Reject. Return to concept stage.

## PART 5 — OGILVY + KOTLER INTEGRATION

Ogilvy's creative philosophy is the "Promotion" quadrant of Kotler's 4 Ps (Kotler & Keller, *Marketing Management* 14th Ed., Ch.10, Books/). The alignment:

| Kotler's Promotion Element | Ogilvy's Rule |
|---------------------------|---------------|
| Advertising | Big Idea + Headline Rule + Long Copy Corollary |
| Sales Promotion | Specific Fact + Promise Principle |
| Public Relations | Brand Image Law (build trust over years, not a sale in a day) |
| Direct Marketing | Long Copy Corollary + Clear CTA |
| Personal Selling | Consumer-Is-Your-Wife Principle (treat the prospect with respect) |

Kotler (Ch.10, pp.439-443): "The promotion mix must be integrated — advertising builds long-term brand equity while sales promotion generates short-term action." Ogilvy understood this distinction without Kotler's academic vocabulary: his "brand image" is the long-term; his "headline that sells" is the short-term. Both must be present.
